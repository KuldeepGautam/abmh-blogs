A brain tumor is an abnormal growth of cells within the brain or central nervous system. These growths can vary greatly in severity and treatment needs.

Introduction to Brain Tumors

What is a brain tumor?

Brain tumors may arise from brain tissue itself (primary tumors) or spread from cancer elsewhere in the body (secondary or metastatic tumors). They can be:

  • Benign (non-cancerous, slow-growing)
  • Malignant (cancerous and potentially life-threatening)
  • Understanding the type and location of a tumor is essential for determining the best treatment.

What Is Brain Tumor Excision?

Brain tumor excision is a surgical procedure to remove abnormal brain tissue. It may involve complete or partial tumor removal depending on its type, size, and location.

When is surgical removal recommended?

Surgery is often advised when:

  • The tumor is accessible and operable
  • It causes symptoms due to pressure on brain structures
  • A tissue sample is required for diagnosis

Goals of the surgery

  • Complete removal of the tumor when possible
  • Biopsy for diagnosis and treatment planning
  • Relief from symptoms like headaches, seizures, or vision changes

Common Types of Brain Tumors Treated

  • Meningiomas
  • Gliomas (astrocytomas, glioblastomas)
  • Pituitary tumors
  • Schwannomas
  • Metastatic tumors

Symptoms That May Require Brain Tumor Surgery

  • Persistent headaches
  • Seizures or unusual movements
  • Vision or hearing loss
  • Nausea/vomiting
  • Cognitive and behavioral changes

Diagnostic Process Before Surgery

  • Neurological examination
  • MRI and CT scans
  • PET scan / SPECT (if required)
  • Biopsy
  • Functional MRI for identifying vital brain areas

Brain Tumor Excision: Procedure Overview

Our surgical approach combines safety, precision, and minimally invasive techniques wherever possible:

  • Pre-Operative Planning using functional imaging and neuronavigation
  • Use of Intraoperative MRI (if available) for real-time accuracy
  • Craniotomy to access the tumor site
  • Microsurgical tools or endoscopic techniques to remove the tumor
  • Closure and post-surgical monitoring
  • Surgery is typically performed under general anesthesia and may last 3–6 hours depending on complexity

Risks and Complications

While brain surgery is generally safe in experienced hands, some risks include:

  • Bleeding or hematoma
  • Infection
  • Brain swelling (edema)
  • Temporary or permanent neurological deficits
  • Seizures

Close monitoring in our advanced neuro-ICU ensures early management of any complications.

Recovery After Brain Tumor Surgery

  • Immediate Post-Operative Care: Includes neuro-monitoring and pain control
  • Physiotherapy & Cognitive Rehabilitation: begins early
  • Return to Normal Life: Varies from 4 weeks to several months

Success Rate and Prognosis

FAQs About Brain Tumor Surgery

1. Is brain tumor excision safe?

Yes, especially when performed using modern tools by experienced surgeons.

2. What happens after removal of a brain tumor?

Post-surgery, patients are monitored closely. Further treatment (radiation/chemo) may be needed for malignant tumors.

3. Will I need radiation or chemotherapy afterward?

Depends on the tumor type. Benign tumors may not need additional therapy.

4. How long is recovery?

Initial recovery is 3–4 weeks, but full recovery may take a few months.

5. Will my cognitive function return?

In many cases, yes—especially with rehabilitation. Some functions may take time to improve.

6. Can you live a normal life after a craniotomy?

Yes, many patients return to work and normal life after recovery.

7. What is the success rate of brain tumor surgery?

It varies but is significantly higher with early detection, benign tumors, and advanced surgical methods.
