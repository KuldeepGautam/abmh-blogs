Introduction to Epilepsy

What is epilepsy?

Epilepsy is a chronic neurological disorder characterized by recurrent seizures—brief episodes of involuntary movement or altered consciousness due to abnormal electrical activity in the brain.

What Causes Epilepsy?

  • Genetic factors
  • Head trauma or brain injury
  • Stroke
  • Brain tumors
  • Infections like meningitis or encephalitis

Types of Seizures:

  • Focal Seizures (start in one area of the brain)
  • Generalized Seizures (involve both hemispheres)
  • Unknown Onset Seizures

What Is Epilepsy Surgery?

Epilepsy surgery involves a neurosurgical procedure to remove or alter brain tissue where seizures originate, helping reduce or eliminate seizures.

When is surgery considered?

Surgery is typically recommended for patients who:

  • Don’t respond to anti-epileptic medications
  • Experience frequent or disabling seizures
  • Have a clearly identified seizure focus

Goals of Surgery:

  • Seizure freedom or significant reduction
  • Improved cognition, mental health, and daily life

Who Is a Candidate for Epilepsy Surgery?

Surgery may be appropriate for patients with:

  • Drug-resistant epilepsy (DRE) – seizures that persist despite trying two or more medications
  • Seizures originating in a localized brain area identifiable on imaging
  • Daily life disruptions due to frequent or severe seizures

Pre-Surgical Evaluation

A thorough evaluation helps determine if surgery is safe and effective for the patient.

Tests and Imaging Include:

  • Video EEG Monitoring to capture seizure activity
  • MRI Brain Scan for structural assessment
  • PET/SPECT Scans to identify seizure foci
  • Neuropsychological Testing for brain function evaluation
  • Functional MRI or Wada Test to assess language and memory areas
  • Visit our Advanced Imaging & Diagnostics section for details.

Types of Epilepsy Surgeries

We offer a wide range of surgical options based on seizure type and location:

  • Resective Surgery: Removal of seizure-causing brain tissue (e.g., temporal lobectomy)
  • Lesionectomy: Removal of tumors or malformations
  • Corpus Callosotomy: Disconnection between brain hemispheres to prevent seizure spread
  • Hemispherectomy: Removal or disconnection of one brain hemisphere (for severe cases)
  • Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS): Implantation of a device that reduces seizure activity
  • Responsive Neurostimulation (RNS): A device that detects and disrupts seizures in real-time

Epilepsy Surgery Procedure Overview

  • Admission and Preparation: Includes routine tests and final evaluation
  • Anesthesia: Usually general
  • Surgical Steps: Depend on the procedure type and brain region involved
  • Duration: Typically 3–6 hours depending on complexity

Benefits of Epilepsy Surgery

  • Significant reduction or complete control of seizures
  • Improved mood, memory, and concentration
  • Reduced dependency on medications
  • Enhanced independence and quality of life

Risks and Complications

Although generally safe, surgery carries some risks:

  • Memory or speech changes (based on surgery location)
  • Infection or bleeding
  • Bleeding
  • Stroke (rare)
  • Temporary neurological deficits

Our team uses neuro-navigation and intraoperative monitoring to minimize these risks.

Recovery After Epilepsy Surgery

  • Hospital Stay: Usually 3–7 days
  • Activity: Gradual resumption over 4–6 weeks
  • Seizure Monitoring: Continued EEGs and follow-ups
  • Support: Includes physiotherapy, cognitive rehab, and psychological care

Life After Epilepsy Surgery

Many patients report a dramatic improvement in life after surgery:

  • Seizure freedom or better seizure control
  • Reduced medications and fewer side effects
  • Ability to drive, continue education, or return to work
  • Support via counseling
