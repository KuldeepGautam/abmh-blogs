Human metapneumovirus (hMPV) is a respiratory virus that causes mild to severe infections in people of all ages. While it typically results in cold-like symptoms, it can lead to severe complications in infants, older adults, and immunocompromised individuals. Early diagnosis, supportive care, and preventive measures such as hygiene practices are key to managing hMPV infection.

Overview

Human metapneumovirus (HMPV) is a common cause of respiratory infections and is closely related to respiratory syncytial virus (RSV). First identified in 2001, hMPV can affect individuals of all age groups, with symptoms ranging from mild cold-like issues to severe respiratory distress, especially in vulnerable populations such as infants, elderly adults, and people with compromised immune systems.

Symptoms

HMPV symptoms vary based on the individual's age, immune status, and underlying health conditions. Common symptoms include:

  • Cold-like symptoms: These include a runny nose, nasal congestion, and sneezing, which are the body’s initial responses to a respiratory infection. The virus irritates the nasal passages, triggering mucus production and sneezing to expel the virus.
  • Cough and sore throat: A cough develops as the body’s reflex to clear mucus or irritants from the airways. A sore throat occurs due to inflammation caused by the virus in the throat or upper respiratory tract. While these are often mild, they can become more intense if the infection worsens.
  • Fever and fatigue: Fever is the body’s way of fighting the virus, as higher temperatures help kill pathogens. Fatigue results from the body using energy to combat the infection, leaving the individual feeling tired or lethargic.
  • Wheezing and shortness of breath: When the virus affects the lower respiratory tract, it causes inflammation and constriction in the airways, leading to wheezing and difficulty breathing. These symptoms are particularly concerning in vulnerable populations, such as young children and older adults.
  • In severe cases: The infection can progress to bronchitis, bacterial lung infections, or pneumonia. These complications occur when the virus spreads deeper into the lungs, causing significant inflammation and impairing oxygen exchange. High-risk individuals, such as those with weakened immune systems or underlying health conditions, are more susceptible to these severe outcomes.

Causes

The HMPV virus spreads primarily through respiratory droplets when an infected person coughs, sneezes, or talks. It can also spread via:

  • Close contact: The virus spreads easily through direct interaction, such as caring for an infected person or being in close proximity while they cough or sneeze. Tiny respiratory droplets carrying the virus can transfer from one person to another during such interactions.
  • Contaminated surfaces: Human metapneumovirus (HMPV) can survive on surfaces. Touching an infected surface, like doorknobs or countertops, and then touching your eyes, nose, or mouth allows the virus to enter the body. This is a common transmission route in public or shared spaces.
  • Seasonal activity: The virus tends to thrive during late winter and early spring, similar to other respiratory infections like influenza. This seasonal pattern may be influenced by colder temperatures and increased indoor gatherings, which create favourable conditions for the virus to spread.

Diagnosis

Diagnosing an HMPV infection involves the following steps:

  • Clinical assessment: A healthcare professional carefully examines the patient's symptoms, such as fever, cough, and breathing difficulties, while reviewing their medical history and potential exposure to respiratory infections. This initial evaluation helps narrow down potential causes.
  • Laboratory tests: Nasal or throat swabs are collected to detect the presence of human metapneumovirus (HMPV). These samples are tested using techniques like PCR (polymerase chain reaction) to confirm the virus.
  • Differential diagnosis: Since HMPV symptoms overlap with other respiratory infections like RSV or influenza, additional tests may be conducted to rule out these conditions and ensure an accurate diagnosis, which is critical for proper management.

Treatment

There is no specific HMPV treatment; management focuses on alleviating symptoms:

  1. Supportive care:
    1. Rest and hydration.
    2. Over-the-counter medications to reduce fever and pain.
  2. Severe cases: Hospitalisation may be required for oxygen therapy or mechanical ventilation in cases of severe respiratory distress.
  3. No antiviral drugs or vaccines: Currently, there are no approved antivirals or vaccines for HMPV.

Prevention

Preventing hMPV in adults and children involves maintaining good hygiene:

  1. Regular handwashing: Use soap and water frequently.
  2. Avoid close contact: Stay away from infected individuals.
  3. Disinfect surfaces: Clean high-touch areas regularly.
  4. Vaccination for other viruses: Ensure flu and RSV vaccines are up-to-date to prevent co-infections.

When to See a Doctor?

It’s essential to seek medical attention if:

  • Symptoms worsen or do not improve after a few days.
  • There is difficulty breathing, wheezing, or persistent high fever.
  • A person in a high-risk group, such as young children, elderly adults, or those with underlying health conditions, contracts the infection.

Conclusion

HMPV is a common but often under-recognised cause of respiratory infections worldwide. While most cases resolve with supportive care, severe complications can arise in vulnerable populations. Adopting preventive measures, recognising symptoms early, and seeking medical care when necessary can minimise the impact of this viral infection.

FAQs

Is human metapneumovirus serious?

For most people, hMPV causes mild respiratory symptoms. However, it can lead to severe complications in infants, older adults, and immunocompromised individuals.

How do I get rid of HMPV?

Treatment is primarily supportive, including rest, hydration, and over-the-counter medications to manage symptoms.

How long does HMPV last?

Symptoms generally last for 1–2 weeks, though they may linger longer in people with severe infections or compromised immune systems.

Is HMPV curable?

There’s no direct cure, but with supportive care like rest, hydration, and managing symptoms, most people recover fully.
