Heart Rate Guide: What’s Normal, What’s Risky, and When to Act

Your heart works like a pump, beating every second of every day to keep blood flowing through your body. The number of times your heart beats in a minute is called your heart rate, and knowing whether yours is in the normal heart rate range can tell you a lot about your health.

In this article, we’ll discuss the normal heart rate by age, factors that affect it, when it can become dangerous, and what steps to take if it’s too high or too low.

What is a Normal Heart Rate?

For most healthy adults, the heart rate normal range is between 60 and 100 beats per minute (bpm). If your heart beats too slowly (bradycardia) or too quickly (tachycardia) without a clear reason, it could be a sign of a health issue that needs attention. And generally, a normal heart rate is measured when you are at rest, also called your “resting heart rate.”

Normal Heart Rate by Age

Heart rate changes with age. Babies and children usually have faster heartbeats because their bodies and organs are still developing. Here’s a general guide to normal heart rate by age:

Age Normal Heart Rate
0 to 1 months 70-190 bpm
1 to 11 months 80-160 bpm
1 to 2 years 80-130 bpm
3 to 5 years 80-120 bpm
6 to 15 years 70-100 bpm
18+ years 60-100 bpm

Please note that athletes or people who exercise regularly may have a resting heart rate as low as 40–60 bpm, which can still be normal if there are no symptoms.

Factors That Affect Heart Rate

Many things can make your heart beat faster or slower, including:

  • Physical activity naturally increases the heart rate.
  • Stress, anxiety, or excitement can speed it up.
  • A fever can sometimes increase heart rate.
  • Some medications lower or raise the heart rate.
  • Conditions like high blood pressure, thyroid problems, or dehydration can affect heart rate.

When is a Heart Rate Dangerous?

A temporary increase during exercise is normal. If your resting heart rate is regularly above 100 bpm (tachycardia) or below 60 bpm (bradycardia) without reason, it could be dangerous.

What is a Dangerous Heart Rate?

  • Too high: Above 120–150 bpm at rest (without exertion) could strain the heart and cause dizziness or chest pain.
  • Too low: Below 50 bpm with symptoms like fatigue, shortness of breath, or fainting may signal a problem.

When to Seek Medical Help?

If you notice the following symptoms along with abnormal heart rate, get medical help immediately:

  • Chest pain or pressure
  • Shortness of breath
  • Fainting or severe dizziness
  • Heart palpitations (feeling like your heart is racing or skipping beats)

This is especially important if you have a history of high blood pressure or heart disease.

Heart Rate and Exercise

When you exercise, your heart rate rises to pump more oxygen-rich blood to your muscles. This is normal, but knowing your target heart rate can help you exercise safely.

Formula: 220 – your age = maximum heart rate.
The safe exercise range is usually 50–85% of your maximum.

Heart Attack During Exercise – Is It Possible?

Yes. While exercise is generally good for heart health, overexertion—especially if you already have risk factors like high blood pressure—can trigger a heart attack during exercise. That’s why it’s important to start gradually and listen to your body.

How to Measure Your Heart Rate

You can check your heart rate by feeling your pulse:

  1. Place your index and middle fingers on your wrist or neck.
  2. Count the beats for 30 seconds and multiply by two.
  3. This number is your beats per minute (bpm).

Fitness trackers and smartwatches can also monitor your heart rate throughout the day.

What to Do If Your Heart Rate Is Too High or Too Low

If your heart rate suddenly spikes above 120 bpm or drops below 50 bpm with symptoms, follow this emergency guide:

  1. Stop all activity and sit or lie down.
  2. Breathe slowly to help calm your heart.
  3. Call emergency services if you have chest pain, fainting, or shortness of breath.
  4. If you have a heart condition, take your prescribed medication (as directed by your doctor).

Keeping Your Heart Rate Healthy

Here are some tips to maintain a healthy heart rate in the normal range:

  • Walking, swimming, or cycling improves heart health.
  • Eat fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.
  • Meditation, yoga, and deep breathing can help.
  • Avoid smoking and excess caffeine, both can raise the heart rate.
  • Routine health checkup helps catch problems early.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. When is a heart rate considered too high?

Your maximum safe heart rate is roughly 220 minus your age. For example, at 35 years old, it’s about 185 bpm. Going above this during exercise can be dangerous.

2. What is the heart rate danger zone?

A heart rate above 120–140 bpm at rest or below 60 bpm (without being an athlete) may be risky and needs immediate medical attention.

3. Is a heart rate of 200 bpm dangerous?

Yes. It may indicate ventricular tachycardia, which can lead to cardiac arrest if prolonged.

4. How can you lower your heart rate quickly?

Practice deep breathing, relax, stay hydrated, splash cold water on your face, and adopt long-term habits like regular exercise, healthy eating, and quitting smoking.

5. What does “heart age” mean?

It’s a measure of how healthy your heart is compared to your actual age, based on factors like lifestyle and health history.
