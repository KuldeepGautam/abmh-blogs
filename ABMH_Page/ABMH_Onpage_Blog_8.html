Diabetes (diabetes mellitus) is a long-term condition where the body either doesn’t make enough insulin or can’t use insulin properly, leading to high blood sugar levels. There are different types of diabetes, such as Type 1, Type 2, and gestational diabetes. The condition is manageable with lifestyle changes and medications, but it can lead to complications if untreated. Early detection and proper care are key.

Diabetes: What It Is, Causes, Symptoms, Treatment & Types

What Is Diabetes?

Diabetes is a condition that affects how your body uses sugar (glucose), which is an essential source of energy. When you eat, your body breaks down food into sugar, which enters the bloodstream. The hormone insulin helps move this sugar from the blood into your cells, where it is used for energy. In diabetes, either your body doesn’t produce enough insulin, or it doesn’t use insulin properly. This causes sugar to build up in your bloodstream.

What Are the Types of Diabetes?

Diabetes comes in several forms, each with its own unique causes and characteristics. Let’s break them down to understand the differences.

1. Insulin Resistance & Pre-diabetes

Before people develop type 2 diabetes, they often go through a stage called Pre-diabetes. At this stage the blood sugar levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be classified as diabetes. This is often due to insulin resistance, which is when the body’s cells start ignoring insulin’s call to absorb sugar from the blood.

Pre-diabetes can turn into Type 2 diabetes if lifestyle changes aren’t made.

The tricky part is that there usually aren't any noticeable symptoms with Pre-diabetes, which is why most people don’t even realize they have it. You might have Pre-diabetes for years without knowing!

2. Type 1 Diabetes

Think of Type 1 diabetes as a mix-up in the immune system. Here, the body’s immune system, which usually defends against infections, accidentally attacks the cells in the pancreas that make insulin. Without insulin, sugar stays in the blood instead of going into the cells for energy. Type 1 is most often seen in children and young adults, though it can develop at any age. Since their bodies can’t make insulin, people with Type 1 diabetes need daily insulin injections.

3. Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes is much more common and typically develops in adults, although it’s becoming more frequent in young people due to lifestyle changes. In this type, the body either doesn’t make enough insulin or doesn’t use it properly, causing blood sugar to rise. Often linked to factors like poor diet, low physical activity, and excess weight, Type 2 diabetes is manageable through lifestyle changes, medications, and sometimes insulin.

4. Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes shows up only during pregnancy. The hormones produced during pregnancy can interfere with insulin, raising blood sugar levels. While it usually disappears after the baby is born, it can increase the mother’s risk of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life. Monitoring and managing blood sugar levels during pregnancy can help reduce risks for both mother and baby.

5. Monogenic Diabetes

Monogenic diabetes is rare and is caused by a mutation in a single gene. It’s usually diagnosed in children or young adults and can sometimes be mistaken for Type 1. However, since the cause is genetic, the treatment can be quite different.

What are the Symptoms of Diabetes?

Diabetes symptoms can appear suddenly or develop gradually, depending on the type. Common diabetes symptoms include:

  • Frequent urination
  • Excessive thirst
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Increased hunger
  • Fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Slow healing of cuts and wounds
  • Tingling or numbness in hands or feet

Symptoms of Type 1 Diabetes

Type 1 diabetes symptoms often appear suddenly and may be severe. In addition to the common symptoms, individuals may experience:

  • Nausea and vomiting – As the body starts breaking down fat for energy, it can lead to the release of chemicals called ketones, which can cause nausea.
  • Rapid weight loss – Type 1 diabetes can cause drastic weight loss in a short period.
  • Breath that smells fruity – This can be due to high ketone levels, especially in a dangerous condition called diabetic ketoacidosis, a medical emergency that requires prompt treatment.

Symptoms of Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes symptoms tend to develop more gradually and may even go unnoticed for a long time. Alongside the common signs of diabetes, individuals might experience:

  • Darkened skin in certain areas – This condition, called acanthosis nigricans, usually affects the neck, armpits, and groin and is a sign of insulin resistance.
  • Frequent infections – Higher blood sugar levels can weaken the immune system, leading to frequent infections, especially of the gums, skin, and bladder.
  • Slow wound healing – Cuts, bruises, and infections may take longer to heal in people with Type 2 diabetes due to poor blood circulation.

Symptoms of Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes often has no noticeable symptoms and is usually discovered through routine screening during pregnancy. However, some women may experience:

  • Increased thirst – Similar to other types, frequent urination can lead to increased thirst.
  • Fatigue – Many women feel tired during pregnancy, but fatigue with gestational diabetes can be more intense.
  • Blurred vision – Higher blood sugar levels can cause swelling in the lenses of the eyes, leading to vision changes.

What Causes Diabetes?

Understanding the causes of diabetes helps in managing and even preventing certain types. Each type of diabetes has unique factors contributing to its development.

• What Causes Type 1 Diabetes?

Type 1 diabetes is generally considered an autoimmune condition. This means the body’s immune system, which normally fights off infections, mistakenly targets and destroys insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. Why this happens isn’t fully understood, but it’s thought that genetics and certain viruses could trigger this response. Although it can happen at any age, Type 1 diabetes is often diagnosed in childhood or young adulthood.

• What Causes Type 2 Diabetes?

The exact cause of Type 2 diabetes isn’t fully known, but it’s usually linked to lifestyle and genetic factors. People who are overweight, eat a diet high in sugar and unhealthy fats, or are physically inactive are at higher risk. Family history and age also play a part, as Type 2 diabetes is more common in those with close relatives who have diabetes and tends to develop with age.

• What Causes Gestational Diabetes?

Gestational diabetes occurs during pregnancy when hormonal changes interfere with the body’s ability to use insulin effectively. The placenta produces hormones to support the baby’s development, but these hormones can sometimes make it harder for insulin to do its job. Women who have a family history of diabetes, are overweight, or are over 25 years old may be more likely to develop gestational diabetes.

• What Else Can Cause Diabetes?

There are other, less common factors that can lead to diabetes. Some medications, hormonal imbalances, and certain diseases of the pancreas can also increase the risk of developing diabetes mellitus. Conditions like polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) and Cushing’s syndrome, which involve hormone imbalances, may also contribute to insulin resistance and blood sugar issues.

What Are the Complications of Diabetes?

When diabetes isn’t managed properly, it can lead to several serious health problems over time. These complications arise because high blood sugar levels affect many parts of the body.

• Heart Disease

People with diabetes are at a higher risk of developing heart problems, including heart attacks, high blood pressure, and clogged arteries. This is because high blood sugar damages blood vessels and increases cholesterol levels, putting more strain on the heart.

• Nerve Damage

Nerve damage, or neuropathy, is common in people with diabetes, especially in the hands and feet. It can cause pain, tingling, or numbness, and can make injuries harder to detect, which might lead to further complications.

• Kidney Failure

The kidneys filter waste from the blood, but high blood sugar can damage their filtering system over time. This can eventually lead to kidney disease or kidney failure, requiring dialysis or even a kidney transplant.

• Eye Problems (Including Blindness)

Diabetes can cause issues like blurry vision, cataracts, glaucoma, and diabetic retinopathy, which damages the blood vessels in the retina. If untreated, these problems can lead to vision loss or blindness.

• Foot Damage

Poor blood flow and nerve damage in the feet can lead to cuts and sores that don’t heal properly. In severe cases, infections can lead to amputations if not treated.

• Skin Infections

People with diabetes are more prone to skin infections, particularly bacterial and fungal infections. High blood sugar can weaken the immune system, making it easier for infections to develop.

• Sexual Health Issues

Diabetes can affect blood flow and nerve function, leading to sexual health issues, such as erectile dysfunction in men and decreased sexual response in women.

• Stroke or Memory Loss

High blood sugar increases the risk of stroke and can also affect brain function, leading to memory problems and even an increased risk of dementia.

Diabetes Diagnosis and Tests

Diagnosing diabetes early is essential to manage and prevent complications. There are several types of tests used to diagnose type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes, Pre-diabetes, and gestational diabetes. These tests help doctors understand your blood sugar levels and whether they fall within normal, prediabetic, or diabetic ranges.

A1C Test

The A1C test gives a picture of your average blood sugar levels over the last two to three months. This is particularly useful as it doesn’t just show a single reading but reflects longer-term blood sugar control. The results are measured in percentages:

  • Normal: Below 5.7%
  • Pre-diabetes: Between 5.7% and 6.4%
  • Diabetes: 6.5% or above

This test doesn’t require fasting, so you can take it at any time of the day. The A1C test is also known as the Haemoglobin A1C or HbA1c test.

Fasting Blood Sugar Test

The fasting blood sugar test measures your blood sugar levels after an overnight fast, meaning you haven’t eaten for at least 8 hours. It’s commonly used to detect type 2 diabetes and Pre-diabetes. Results are measured in mg/dL (milligrams per deciliter):

  • Normal: 99 mg/dL or below
  • Pre-diabetes: Between 100 and 125 mg/dL
  • Diabetes: 126 mg/dL or above

Fasting is required for this test to ensure that the results accurately reflect your blood sugar without the influence of recent food intake.

Glucose Tolerance Test

This test measures your blood sugar levels before and after you drink a sugary liquid. You’ll fast overnight, have your blood drawn to measure your fasting blood sugar, drink the liquid, and then have your blood sugar tested at intervals, usually after 1, 2, and possibly 3 hours.

For the 2-hour mark, the results are:

  • Normal: 140 mg/dL or below
  • Pre-diabetes: Between 140 and 199 mg/dL
  • Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or above

This test is particularly useful for diagnosing type 2 diabetes and gestational diabetes.

Random Blood Sugar Test

Unlike other tests, this one doesn’t require fasting, so it can be done at any time. It checks your blood sugar level at a random time of day, making it convenient for quick diagnosis.

  • Diabetes: 200 mg/dL or above

This test may be used as an initial screening, and if high blood sugar levels are detected, other tests might be recommended to confirm diabetes.

Tests for Gestational Diabetes

For pregnant women, testing for gestational diabetes is essential as it affects both the mother’s and baby’s health.

  • Glucose Screening Test: In this test, you drink a glucose-rich liquid, and after an hour, your blood sugar level is measured. If it’s 140 mg/dL or below, the results are normal. If the level is higher than 140 mg/dL, you may need to take a glucose tolerance test for more accurate results.
  • Glucose Tolerance Test: Similar to the test for type 2 diabetes, you’ll fast overnight and then have your blood sugar levels checked after drinking a glucose solution. Your levels are tested after 1, 2, and possibly 3 hours. Since the glucose drink and testing intervals may vary, it’s important to discuss your specific results with your doctor to understand what they mean.

Diabetes Treatment

The treatment for diabetes varies based on the type of diabetes you have:

• Type 1 Diabetes

People with Type 1 diabetes need daily insulin injections. Since their bodies don’t produce insulin, these injections help control blood sugar levels. Insulin can be administered using syringes, insulin pens, or insulin pumps.

• Type 2 Diabetes

Managing Type 2 diabetes often starts with lifestyle changes, like improving your diet and increasing physical activity. These changes can help lower blood sugar levels. In some cases, medications may be prescribed to help the body use insulin more effectively. If blood sugar levels remain high despite these measures, insulin may also be needed.

• Gestational Diabetes

Gestational diabetes is typically managed through diet and exercise. Women are encouraged to eat healthy foods and stay active. However, some may need insulin or other medications if blood sugar levels don’t stay within the target range. Regular check-ups are important to monitor both the mother’s and the baby’s health.

How is Diabetes Managed?

Managing diabetes requires ongoing care, including:

  • Monitoring blood sugar levels regularly.
  • Taking prescribed medications or insulin.
  • Eating a balanced diet with controlled portions.
  • Exercising regularly to help your body use insulin more effectively.
  • Attending regular check-ups with your doctor.

How to Prevent Diabetes?

Preventing Type 2 diabetes involves lifestyle changes:

  • Maintain a healthy weight.
  • Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.
  • Exercise regularly.
  • Avoid sugary drinks and processed foods.
  • Get regular health screenings if you have a family history of diabetes.

While Type 1 diabetes cannot be prevented, following these steps can lower your risk of diabetes mellitus and improve overall health.

Healthy Living with Diabetes

Living with diabetes involves making health-conscious choices every day. Stick to your medication plan, stay active, eat well, and regularly monitor your blood sugar. Many people with diabetes live long, healthy lives with proper management.

When to See a Doctor?

If you experience any of the diabetes symptoms like excessive thirst, frequent urination, or fatigue, it’s important to consult a doctor. If you have a family history of diabetes, regular check-ups are essential to catch the condition early.

FAQs – Diabetes

How Common is Diabetes?

Diabetes is extremely common, affecting millions of people in India. It’s important to spread awareness to help more people manage and prevent this condition.

How Does Diabetes Affect Individuals?

Diabetes affects individuals by disrupting their body’s ability to regulate blood sugar, leading to potential complications like heart disease, nerve damage, and kidney failure.

What Foods Cause Diabetes?

No specific food directly causes diabetes, but diets high in sugar, processed foods, and unhealthy fats can increase the risk of Type 2 diabetes.

What is the Biggest Cause of Diabetes?

The biggest cause of Type 2 diabetes is a combination of poor lifestyle choices, such as a high-calorie diet, lack of exercise, and obesity. For Type 1, it’s mostly due to genetic factors and autoimmune responses.
