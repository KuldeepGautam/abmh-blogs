Blood cancer refers to cancers affecting the blood, bone marrow, or lymphatic system, including leukaemia, lymphoma, and myeloma. It is caused by genetic mutations and influenced by factors like age, infections, and exposure to harmful chemicals. Common blood cancer symptoms include fatigue, weight loss, bruising, and frequent infections. Early detection and treatment improve survival rates.

Understanding Blood Cancer

Blood cancer is a term used to describe cancers that affect the blood, bone marrow, or lymphatic system, disrupting the normal production and functioning of blood cells. The common types of blood cancer include leukaemia, which originates in the bone marrow and affects white blood cells; lymphoma, a cancer of the lymphatic system; and myeloma, which targets plasma cells and weakens the immune system.

The disease develops when genetic mutations lead to the uncontrolled growth of abnormal blood cells. These cells interfere with the production of healthy blood cells, which are essential for fighting infections, carrying oxygen, and preventing bleeding.

Types of Blood Cancer

Blood cancer is not a single disease but an umbrella term for several types of cancer affecting the blood and lymphatic systems. Here are the most common types of blood cancer:

1. Leukaemia

This cancer originates in the bone marrow and results in the overproduction of abnormal white blood cells, disrupting the body's ability to fight infections.

Common subtypes: Acute lymphocytic leukaemia (ALL), chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL), acute myeloid leukaemia (AML), and chronic myeloid leukaemia (CML).

2. Lymphoma

Lymphoma affects the lymphatic system, which includes lymph nodes and immune cells. It hampers the body’s immune response by creating abnormal lymphocytes.

Common subtypes: Hodgkin lymphoma and Non-Hodgkin lymphoma.

3. Myeloma

Myeloma targets plasma cells, a type of white blood cell responsible for producing antibodies. The condition weakens the immune system and increases susceptibility to infections.

4. Myelodysplastic Syndromes (MDS)

This type affects the bone marrow, leading to inefficient blood cell production and an increased risk of developing acute leukaemia.

5. Myeloproliferative Neoplasms (MPN)

MPN involves the excessive production of blood cells, leading to complications such as blood clots or anaemia.

Blood Cancer Symptoms

Recognizing blood cancer symptoms early is crucial for timely diagnosis and treatment. The symptoms may vary depending on the type but often include:

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Persistent fatigue that doesn’t improve with rest
  • Frequent infections or slow recovery from illnesses
  • Bruising or bleeding easily
  • Night sweats or fever without an apparent cause
  • Swelling or lumps in lymph nodes, particularly in the neck, armpits, or groin
  • Bone pain or tenderness, especially in the back or ribs
  • Pale skin (anaemia) or shortness of breath

If you experience any of these signs for an extended period, consult a healthcare professional promptly.

Why Blood Cancer Occurs

Understanding why blood cancer occurs involves examining genetic and environmental factors:

1. Genetic Mutations

Blood cancer begins with mutations in the DNA of blood cells, causing them to grow and divide uncontrollably. These mutations are often acquired over a person’s lifetime rather than inherited.

2. Risk Factors

    1. Age: The risk increases with age, especially for leukaemia and myeloma.
    2. Exposure to radiation or chemicals: Prolonged exposure to benzene or radiation may elevate risks.
    3. Infections: Viruses like Epstein-Barr or HIV can increase lymphoma risk.
    4. Other factors: Smoking, obesity, and pre-existing blood disorders also play a role.

While the exact cause is not always clear, these factors can influence the likelihood of developing blood cancer.

Diagnosis

Accurate diagnosis is vital to determine the type and stage of blood cancer. Common diagnostic methods include:

1. Complete Blood Count (CBC)

A routine CBC can identify abnormal levels of red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets.

2. Bone Marrow Biopsy

This procedure involves extracting bone marrow to detect cancerous cells.

3. Imaging Tests

X-rays, CT scans, and PET scans help identify swollen lymph nodes or tumours.

4. Flow Cytometry

This test analyses cell markers on the surface of blood cells, helping to classify the type of cancer.

Blood Cancer Treatment

The treatment approach depends on the type, stage, and progression of cancer, as well as the patient's age and overall health.

Common blood cancer treatments include:

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses powerful drugs to kill or slow the growth of cancer cells. It’s highly effective for many blood cancers but can cause side effects like fatigue or nausea.

Radiation Therapy

Radiation therapy uses high-energy rays to destroy cancer cells, often paired with chemotherapy or as preparation for a stem cell transplant.

Targeted Therapies

These modern drugs focus on specific cancer cell mechanisms, reducing harm to healthy cells and minimizing side effects compared to traditional treatments.

Stem Cell Transplants

This procedure replaces diseased bone marrow with healthy stem cells, restoring normal blood production. Stem cells can come from the patient or a donor.

Immunotherapy

Immunotherapy strengthens the immune system’s ability to detect and destroy cancer cells, providing a natural way to fight the disease.

Early intervention and tailored treatments often lead to better outcomes. The survival rates vary, with some patients achieving long-term remission.

When to See a Doctor?

You should consult a doctor if you experience persistent symptoms such as unexplained fatigue, significant weight loss, or frequent infections. Early diagnosis can improve treatment outcomes.
