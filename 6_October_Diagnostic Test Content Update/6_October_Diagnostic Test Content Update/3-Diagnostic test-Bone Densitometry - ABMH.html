What is Bone Densitometry?

Bone densitometry, also known as a DEXA scan, is a simple and painless test that measures how strong your bones are. It helps doctors find out if your bones are healthy or if they are becoming weak, a condition often linked to osteoporosis.

The test uses a very low dose of X-rays to check the density (thickness and strength) of bones, usually in the lower spine and hips, where fractures are most common.

Why is Bone Densitometry Done?

Doctors recommend this test when they want to know your risk of bone fractures or conditions like osteoporosis. It is especially important for:

  • Women after menopause, when bone loss speeds up.
  • Men and women over 50 who have had fractures from minor injuries.
  • People with a family history of osteoporosis.
  • Patients who take long-term medications like steroids, which can weaken bones.
  • People with medical conditions such as thyroid or parathyroid disorders, or chronic diseases affecting bones.

Bone densitometry is also used to monitor how well treatment for bone loss is working.

How is the Bone Densitometry Test Done?

The procedure is quick and does not hurt. Here’s what usually happens:

  • You will lie on a padded table.
  • A scanning arm slowly passes over your body while you stay still.
  • The machine measures the density of your bones in the hip, spine, or sometimes the forearm.
  • The whole process usually takes about 15–20 minutes.

Unlike a normal X-ray, the radiation used in bone densitometry is extremely low and considered very safe.

Benefits of Bone Densitometry

  • Early Detection: Finds bone loss before fractures happen.
  • Accurate Measurement: Helps doctors know your exact bone strength.
  • Safe and Quick: No pain, no needles, and very low radiation.
  • Track Progress: Useful to monitor if your bone-strengthening treatment is working.
  • Prevention: Helps reduce the chances of serious fractures in the future.

Preparation Before the Test

  • Wear comfortable clothing without metal zippers or buttons.
  • Avoid calcium supplements 24 hours before the test.
  • Inform your doctor if you are pregnant.
  • If you have had a barium exam, CT scan with contrast, or nuclear medicine test recently, let your doctor know, as you may need to wait a few days before doing a bone densitometry.

No fasting or injections are required for this test.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Is bone densitometry painful?

No, the test is completely painless and non-invasive.

2. How long does the test take?

It usually takes 15–20 minutes.

3. Is the test safe?

Yes, the radiation exposure is very minimal, much lower than a regular chest X-ray.

4. Who should get a bone densitometry test?

Postmenopausal women, older adults, people on long-term steroids, and those with risk factors for weak bones should get tested.

5. Can children take this test?

Bone densitometry is mainly recommended for adults, but in rare cases, children with certain bone disorders may also need it.

6. How often should the test be repeated?

Usually once every 1–2 years, depending on your doctor’s advice and treatment progress.
