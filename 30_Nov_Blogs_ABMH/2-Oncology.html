Understanding Cancer: A Brief Overview

Cancer begins when abnormal cells grow uncontrollably, forming tumours or spreading to other parts of the body. This process is known as metastasis. These cells disrupt normal functions, but with early detection and timely treatment, cancer can often be controlled or even cured.

Advances in medicine mean that survival rates are higher than ever. With comprehensive care that includes prevention, diagnosis, treatment and long-term support, patients can look forward to not just living but thriving after cancer.

Common Types of Cancer

There are more than 100 diseases, each with unique characteristics and treatments. Here are some common types you should know about:

Cervical Cancer

Cervical cancer affects the cervix, the lower part of the uterus and is often linked to persistent infection with the human papillomavirus (HPV). Regular cancer screening, including Pap smears and HPV testing, can detect precancerous changes early, when treatment is most effective.

Key symptoms: Abnormal vaginal bleeding, unusual discharge and pelvic pain.

Prevention tip: HPV vaccination and regular screenings are powerful ways to reduce your risk.

Blood Cancer

Unlike solid tumours, blood cancers such as Leukaemia, Lymphoma and Myeloma affect blood cells and the bone marrow. They weaken the immune system and interfere with normal blood production.

Key symptoms: Unexplained fatigue, frequent infections, easy bruising and weight loss.

Treatment options: Depending on the type and stage, treatment may include chemotherapy, targeted therapy or stem cell transplantation.

Rectal Cancer

Rectal cancer originates in the rectum, the final section of the large intestine. Early detection through screenings like colonoscopy is key to improving outcomes.

Key symptoms: Changes in bowel habits, blood in stool, abdominal discomfort and unexplained weight loss.

Treatment options: Surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy or a combination of these approaches.

The Power of Early Detection and Cancer Screening

Early detection saves lives. Many cancers grow silently in their initial stages, showing little to no symptoms. Regular cancer screening can identify changes long before they become life-threatening, allowing for simpler, more effective treatments.

Important screening tests include:

  • Pap smears and HPV testing for cervical cancer
  • Blood tests and bone marrow exams for certain blood cancers
  • Colonoscopy for colorectal and rectal cancers
  • Imaging tests like CT scans, MRIs and PET scans to detect tumours and monitor progress

Even if you feel healthy, routine check-ups are essential, especially if you have risk factors like a family history of cancer, smoking or prolonged exposure to harmful substances.

Cancer Treatment: Precision and Personalisation

No two cancers are the same, and neither are their treatments. Today’s cancer care is highly personalised, with plans tailored to each patient’s type, stage and overall health. Here are the most common treatment options:

  • Surgery: Surgery aims to remove cancerous tissue and is often most effective when cancer is localised.
  • Chemotherapy: Chemotherapy uses strong medications to destroy rapidly dividing cancer cells. It may be used alone or in combination with other treatments.
  • Radiation Therapy: Radiation therapy targets and kills cancer cells using high-energy rays. It’s often used before surgery to shrink tumours or afterwards to eliminate remaining cells.
  • Targeted Therapy: This precise treatment focuses on specific molecules driving cancer growth, often causing fewer side effects than chemotherapy.
  • Immunotherapy: A revolutionary approach, immunotherapy boosts the body’s own immune system to fight cancer more effectively, particularly in blood cancers and certain advanced tumours.
  • Palliative Care: Comprehensive cancer care also focuses on quality of life. Palliative care addresses symptoms, manages side effects and supports emotional and mental well-being throughout treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Can cancer be prevented?

While not all cancers are preventable, you can greatly reduce your risk through healthy lifestyle choices. Avoid tobacco, maintain a healthy weight, exercise regularly, limit alcohol, protect your skin from sun exposure and stay current with cancer screening and vaccinations such as the HPV vaccine.

2. How long does cancer treatment take?

The duration varies widely depending on the type and stage of cancer and the treatment plan. Some treatments may last a few weeks, while others continue for months or years. Your oncologist will guide you on the expected timeline for your situation.

3. Are all cancer treatments painful?

Modern cancer care uses advanced pain-management techniques. While some treatments may cause temporary discomfort, your care team will work to minimise pain and side effects throughout your journey.

4. What’s the success rate of cancer treatment?

Success rates depend on many factors, including cancer type, stage and individual health. Many cancers detected early have high cure rates. Your doctor can provide personalized information about your prognosis.
