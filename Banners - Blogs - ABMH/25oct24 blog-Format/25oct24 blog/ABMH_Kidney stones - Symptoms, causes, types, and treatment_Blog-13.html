Everything You Need to Know About Kidney Stones—Symptoms, Causes, Types, and Treatment

A kidney stone is a hard deposit made of minerals and salts that form inside your kidneys. These stones’ size can vary from a grain of sand to a large golf ball. They can develop anywhere in your urinary system, including the kidney, bladder, and urethra. Therefore, it’s important to recognize their symptoms and get the right treatment quickly.

Are You Experiencing Severe Kidney Stone Symptoms?

If you're experiencing any of the following symptoms, seek medical help right away:

  • Severe or sharp pain in your back, side, lower belly, or groin
  • Blood in your urine
  • Cloudy or foul-smelling urine
  • Feeling nauseous or vomiting
  • High fever and chills
  • Constant feeling of needing to urinate but passing little or no urine

These symptoms could indicate that your kidney stone is causing serious problems, such as a blockage or infection. If you're dealing with any of these symptoms, don't delay!

What is a Kidney Stone?

The kidneys are two bean-shaped organs found below your rib cage, on each side of your spine. Their job is to filter waste and extra water from your blood, creating urine. The urine then moves through two thin tubes, called ureters, into the bladder.

Besides filtering waste, the kidneys help control the levels of water, salt, and minerals in your blood. When there’s too much of these minerals in the urine, it leads to the formation of kidney stones, also known as renal calculi.

Kidney Stone Symptoms

Kidney stone symptoms generally depend on the size of the kidney stone. Smaller kidney stones might not cause any symptoms at all. However, larger kidney stones can get stuck in your urinary tract, causing pain and other issues. They can lead to symptoms such as:

  • Severe or sharp pain in the side and back, below the ribs
  • Pain that radiates to the lower abdomen and groin
  • Pain or burning sensation while urinating
  • Blood in your urine
  • Bad-smelling or cloudy urine
  • Constant need to pee all the time
  • Constant stomach pain
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Fever and chills (this can happen if the stone causes an infection)

Pain caused by kidney stones might change. For instance, the pain might shift from one location to another or increase in intensity. If you experience extreme pain, make an appointment with your doctor.

Seek immediate medical attention if you experience these symptoms:

  • Severe pain that restricts your movement
  • Pain accompanied by nausea and vomiting
  • Pain accompanied by fever and chills
  • Extreme difficulty passing urine

What Causes Kidney Stone?

Here are some probable reasons for kidney stones:

  1. Family or Personal History: If a family member has had kidney stones, your chances of getting them are higher too. Additionally, if you’ve already had kidney stones, you’re at greater risk of having more in the future.
  2. Dehydration:Not drinking enough water can increase your risk of kidney stones. People living in hot, dry areas or those who sweat a lot may be at an even higher risk.
  3. Diet: A diet high in protein, salt, and sugar can raise your risk of kidney stones, especially if it's particularly salty. Too much salt makes your kidneys filter more calcium, raising the chance of stones forming.
  4. Obesity: Having a high Body Mass Index (BMI), a large waist, or gaining weight can all be linked to a higher risk of kidney stones.
  5. Digestive Diseases and Surgery: Conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, gastric bypass surgery, or chronic diarrhea can affect how your body absorbs calcium and water. This can lead to higher levels of substances that form stones in your urine.

Types of Kidney Stones

Understanding the type of kidney stone is important because it can help you take the right preventative steps. Here are the main types of kidney stones:

  1. Calcium Stones:

    Most kidney stones are calcium stones, typically formed from calcium oxalate, which is produced by your liver. It can also come from foods like certain fruits, vegetables, nuts, and chocolate. Some of the factors that contribute to the high level of calcium or oxalate in your urine are—excessive Vitamin D intake, intestinal surgery, and metabolic disorders.

  2. Struvite Stones:

    These stones form due to a urinary tract infection. They can grow rapidly and become quite large, often with few noticeable symptoms.

  3. Uric Acid Stones:

    Uric acid stones can form in people who lose a lot of fluids from chronic diarrhea or malabsorption, those on a high-protein diet, and individuals with diabetes or metabolic syndrome. Genetics can also raise the risk of these stones.

  4. Cystine Stones:

    These stones form in people with a genetic condition called cystinuria, which causes the kidneys to release too much of a certain amino acid.

Treatment for Kidney Stone

There are different ways to treat a kidney stone. However, the treatment for kidney stones depends on factors such as:

  • Size of the stone
  • Type of stone
  • Location of the stone
  • Whether the stone is causing pain, and more

If the kidney stone is small and can pass on its own, your doctor may suggest drinking plenty of fluids to help flush the stone out. Most kidney stones that are less than 5 millimeters, and about half of the stones between 5 and 10 millimeters, can leave the body on their own. A doctor might also recommend pain relief medicine to ease any discomfort.

For larger stones or if the stone is blocking your urinary tract and causing pain, your doctor may suggest treatments to break the stone into smaller pieces or to remove it surgically.

Here are the main treatment options:

  1. Lithotripsy: This treatment uses shock waves to break the kidney stone into tiny pieces. The small fragments can then pass through your urinary tract and be eliminated through urine. The procedure usually lasts about an hour and is often done under general anesthesia, which means you'll be asleep and won't feel anything. Most people can go home the same day.
  2. Ureteroscopy: In this procedure, a doctor uses a long, tube-like instrument that goes through the genitals and bladder into your ureter to remove the stone (if it’s small) or break it into smaller pieces using a laser (if it’s larger). If the stone is in the bladder, they might use a tool called a cystoscope. This procedure is done under general anesthesia.
  3. Surgery: If the stone is too large or in a difficult location, your doctor might suggest a more serious surgery called Percutaneous Nephrolithotomy. This involves inserting a tube through a small cut in your back to reach the kidneys and remove the stone. If the stone is very large, a laser may be used to break it into smaller pieces. This procedure requires anesthesia and usually involves a few days in the hospital afterwards.

Conclusion

Kidney stones affect more than 1 in 10 people. They can be very painful and can also lead to more serious problems like kidney infections or even kidney failure if not treated.
