First Aid for Stroke: What You Should Know

A stroke is a medical emergency that requires immediate attention. Recognising the signs early and administering proper first aid can make a lot of difference between life and death or between full recovery and long-term disability. Knowing stroke first aid is critical whether you're at home, work, or in a public space.

In this guide, we’ll break down what a stroke is, how to identify it, what first aid steps to take, and where to get emergency care.

What Causes a Stroke?

Before providing first aid for a stroke, it’s important to understand what actually triggers one. A stroke happens when blood can't reach a part of the brain like it should. Without enough blood, the brain doesn’t get the oxygen and nutrients it needs. This can quickly cause brain cells to become damaged or die within minutes.

Strokes typically fall into two categories:

  • Ischemic Stroke: This is the most common kind. It happens when a blood clot blocks or narrows a blood vessel in the brain. This stops blood from reaching certain parts of the brain, and those areas start to suffer because they don’t get the oxygen and nutrients they need.
  • Hemorrhagic Stroke: This type of stroke occurs when a blood vessel in the brain breaks open and causes bleeding in the surrounding brain tissue. The buildup of blood creates pressure inside the skull, which can harm brain cells and lead to serious damage.

Both forms of stroke are medical emergencies that demand immediate attention and treatment.

Recognising the Signs of a Stroke

The faster a stroke is recognized and treated, the better the chances of preventing serious brain damage or even death. That’s why it’s so important to know the signs. A simple way to remember them is the BE FAST method. Each letter stands for a symptom you should watch for:

  • B – Balance: Sudden trouble with balance, coordination, or dizziness. The person may stumble or have trouble standing or walking steadily.
  • E – Eyes: Sudden changes in vision—such as blurry sight, seeing double, or unexpectedly losing vision in one of the eyes or both eyes—can be a sign of a stroke.
  • F – Face: One side of the face may look uneven or start drooping. Ask the person to smile—if it’s lopsided, that’s a red flag.
  • A – Arms: Have the person try lifting both arms. If one arm drops down, feels weak, or they can’t move it properly, this could be a sign of a stroke.
  • S – Speech: Pay attention to how the person speaks. If their speech sounds slurred, unusually slow, or hard to understand, or if they struggle to find the right words or follow what you're saying, it could be a warning sign of a stroke.
  • T – Time: Time is critical. If any of these signs appear, call emergency services right away. Do not wait to see if symptoms get better.

Even if the symptoms go away after a few minutes, take it seriously. This could be a transient ischemic attack (TIA)—sometimes called a mini-stroke. A TIA doesn’t cause permanent damage, but it's often a warning sign that a major stroke could happen soon. Getting medical help immediately can prevent something much worse.

Stroke First Aid: Immediate Steps

Here’s what to do when someone shows signs of a stroke:

1. Call for Emergency Help Immediately

Dial emergency services. In India, dial 108 or your local ambulance number. While you wait, do not try to drive the person yourself unless absolutely necessary.

2. Keep the Person Safe and Comfortable

Lay the person down on their side with their head slightly elevated if they're conscious. This position helps prevent choking if they vomit and improves blood flow to the brain.

3. Don’t Offer Food, Water, or Medication

Swallowing can be impaired during a stroke, increasing the risk of choking. Never give food, drinks, or pills—even if they ask for them.

4. Note the Time

Make a mental note of when symptoms began. Emergency medicine doctors use this information to decide which treatments are safe and effective, especially for clot-busting drugs.

5. Stay Calm and Reassuring

A stroke is terrifying for the person experiencing it. Speak calmly, offer reassurance, and monitor their breathing and consciousness until help arrives.

What Not to Do

  • Don’t delay calling emergency services.
  • Don’t let the person sleep or “wait it out.”
  • Don’t assume the symptoms will go away.
  • Don’t give aspirin if it’s a haemorrhagic stroke—it can make bleeding worse.

First Aid and Stroke Recovery

Quick and correct first aid doesn't just save a life—it can also improve the chances of a strong recovery. Prompt treatment reduces the amount of brain damage and speeds up rehabilitation.

Survivors who receive treatment within the golden window (typically within 3 to 4.5 hours) often experience better outcomes. Stroke recovery starts with the first steps of first aid and continues through hospital treatment, rehabilitation, and lifestyle changes.

When to Head to the Hospital

Even if symptoms are mild or disappear quickly, a medical evaluation is essential. A minor stroke today could be followed by a major one tomorrow. Head to a hospital equipped to handle neurological emergencies.

Prevention is Still Key

While first aid saves lives, stroke prevention saves futures. Here are simple ways to lower your risk:

  • Control Blood Pressure and Cholesterol: Regular checkups help keep these in check.
  • Quit Smoking and Limit Alcohol: Major contributors to stroke risk.
  • Stay Active and Eat Well: A balanced diet and daily movement go a long way.
  • Manage Diabetes and Heart Conditions: Proper management reduces the risk of stroke.
